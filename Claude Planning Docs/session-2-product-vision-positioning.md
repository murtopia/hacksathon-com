## Overview

This document defines the product vision and market positioning for Hacksathon.com, based on the Seven2 Hacks-a-Thon case study and learnings synthesis from Session 1. It is intended as a standing reference throughout product development.

---

## 1. Ideal Customer Profile

### Company type
Creative and knowledge-work agencies. 20–200 person companies. Strategy, design, marketing, PR, consulting. Non-technical teams where even PMs don't code.

### Team composition
Producers, designers, strategists, and project managers. 1–2 technical people at most. Leadership that is curious about AI capability and wants proof before commitment.

### Trigger moment
"We need to show our team what AI can do." Often arises from post-offsite momentum, a leadership mandate, or competitive pressure. The buyer wants a structured experience, not a free-for-all.

### Budget signal
$3k–$15k event budget. Would pay for facilitation, platform access, and a repeatable format. Not looking for a DIY tool — wants a done-with-you experience.

---

## 2. Unique Value Proposition vs. Running a Hackathon Manually

Running a hackathon manually means duct-taping Notion docs, Slack channels, Google Forms, and Zoom calls together — then hoping someone has the energy to synthesize the results. The format only works if one heroic facilitator holds it all together. When they leave, the institutional knowledge leaves too.

| Manual hackathon | Hacksathon.com |
|---|---|
| Format lives in a PDF playbook | Format IS the product — built into every block |
| Ideas captured in a spreadsheet | IdeaLab with PRD generation, voting, and status tracking |
| Planning is skipped because no one enforces it | Planning is a required, guided step before any build |
| Pitch quality depends on the room | Pitch structure is coached in the platform |
| Results disappear after demo day | Post-event continuation path and idea bank persist |
| Second event requires starting from scratch | Each event compounds into org-level AI capability data |

### The core differentiator
No platform exists that combines structured facilitation format + AI-native build tooling + non-technical team focus + commercial repeatability. That is the whitespace Hacksathon.com occupies.

## 3. Competitive Landscape

### Devpost
- **Built for:** Developer hackathons, sponsor-led events, public submissions
- **Core weakness:** Assumes technical participants. No facilitation. Pure submission infrastructure.
- **Our position:** We facilitate non-technical teams

### MLH (Major League Hacking)
- **Built for:** Student developers, university events, in-person competitions
- **Core weakness:** Community and credentialing focus. Zero internal-team / corporate use case.
- **Our position:** We serve companies, not colleges

### HackerEarth
- **Built for:** Enterprise recruitment, engineering team assessments
- **Core weakness:** Evaluation-first mindset. Intimidating to non-technical participants. Not about building culture.
- **Our position:** We are about creative unlocking, not assessment

### Internal playbooks (DIY)
- **Built for:** One-off internal innovation events run by L&D or leadership
- **Core weakness:** Format dies with the facilitator. No tooling. Can't scale or repeat reliably.
- **Our position:** We make the format portable and repeatable

## 4. Why This Is Specifically Powerful for Non-Technical Teams

### The intimidation gap is the product opportunity
Non-technical teams don't know what they can build. The moment they ship their first working app — even a simple one — the ceiling lifts permanently. That moment is designed into every block of the format.

### Passion projects outperform work projects
When people build something they personally want to exist, engagement and quality spike. Non-technical teams are full of these ideas. They just didn't think they were allowed to build them. The Seven2 top three award winners — Drift (bedtime stories), Cut-up Lyric Generator, Even Grounds (coffee journaling) — were all deeply personal.

### Structure removes the fear barrier
Time-blocked sessions, a defined pitch format, and a clear demo day create accountability without pressure. Non-technical participants need the container more than technical ones do. The structure is the product — the tools are secondary.

### The mindset shift is the durable outcome
"Someone should make an app for that" becomes "I could build that." That cognitive shift — documented in 9 of 13 Seven2 reflections — is the real ROI a CMO can sell to their CEO.

## 5. Pitch Hierarchy

### One-line pitch
The structured hackathon format that turns non-technical teams into builders — in six weeks, part-time.

### Elevator pitch (30 seconds)
Most companies want their teams to understand AI by using it — not by sitting through another workshop. Hacksathon gives you a complete format: a facilitated, time-blocked build program where anyone — regardless of technical background — ships a real, working product. We ran it at a 22-person creative agency. 14 people shipped live apps in six weeks. None of them had ever written code.

### Positioning statement
For creative and knowledge-work teams who want to build genuine AI capability without a technical team, Hacksathon is the only structured hackathon platform designed specifically for non-developers — combining guided facilitation, AI-native build tools, and a repeatable format that turns intimidation into shipped products. Unlike Devpost or DIY Notion playbooks, Hacksathon makes the format itself the product, so companies can run it again and again without starting from scratch.

## 6. Brand Personality

### North star phrase
*"Build something you personally want to exist in the world."*

This is the first thing every participant hears. Before tools, before format, before anything else. It is the permission structure the entire experience rests on.

### Earned confidence, not hype
We don't promise transformation. We promise a first live URL. The confidence comes from actually shipping, not from marketing. "We're all just hacks" is the energy — self-aware, unpretentious, genuinely excited.

### Low pressure, high accountability
The format creates structure without judgment. Progress over perfection is the law. But "in progress" is never the final state — there is a demo day, and you will stand up in front of your peers.

### Celebration is built in
The awards ceremony isn't an afterthought. It's the emotional payoff of the whole format. Categories like "Shut Up and Take My Money" and "Most [Company] Energy" make it feel human, not corporate.

### AI as collaborator, not magic
The platform language is always "your AI partner," not "our AI engine." We don't overclaim. We help skeptics find the one axis — productivity, prototyping — where AI earns their trust without asking them to resolve the broader ethical debate. Don't try to convert; just demonstrate.

### Tool-agnostic, format-first
We don't evangelize Lovable or any single tool. The format works with Cursor, Bolt, Replit, whatever comes next. Organizers choose their stack. We provide the rails.

### Emotional arc (the metric that matters most)
> Intimidation → Permission → First output → Surprise → Pride → "I have more ideas"

Design the platform to maximize each transition point.

## 7. The Seven2 Case Study — Proof of Concept

### Key metrics

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Participants | ~22 |
| Live apps shipped | 14 |
| Completion rate | ~64% |
| Prior coding experience | ~0 (2 participants with light technical background) |
| Reflection submissions | 13 |
| Participants wanting to continue building | 9 of 13 (69%) |
| Event duration | ~6 weeks, part-time, 30–45 min sessions |
| Best in Show margin | 12 of 15 votes (80%) |

### How to frame the case study

Don't lead with the tools. Lead with the people. A creative director who made bedtime stories for her kids. A strategist who built a coffee journaling app. A project manager who shipped a cybersecurity onboarding tool. These are the proof points — not the completion rate.

### Suggested case study structure

**The Challenge** — Creative agency, 22 people, wanted AI fluency without a developer hire.

**The Format** — 6-week, part-time, time-blocked sessions. Shark Tank pitch. Demo day. Awards.

**The Results** — 64% completion rate, 14 live apps, unanimous surprise at accessibility.

**The Unexpected Win** — The top projects were personal passion projects, not work tools. The emotional resonance of building something you actually want to exist produced the highest quality outcomes.

**The Lasting Impact** — 69% of participants left wanting to keep building. The event created a capability shift, not just a one-time output.

### The two audiences for the case study

**For buyers (CMOs, agency heads):** The headline stat is 64% completion and zero coding background. In traditional developer hackathons, many technical teams fail to ship. These weren't technical teams.

**For participants:** The mindset shift quote — *"I have definitely found myself thinking, 'oh I could make a solution for that' — rather than 'someone should make an app for that.'"* — Sena Lauer, Seven2.

## Key Product Implications — Top 5

1. **The format IS the product.** The time-blocked, low-pressure, passion-project structure is what produced the outcomes. Every feature should serve the format, not distract from it.

2. **The emotional arc is the key metric.** Design the platform to maximize each transition from intimidation to pride. The "I can't believe I just did that" moment should be as early and as inevitable as possible.

3. **Planning before prompting is the highest-leverage intervention.** Make planning unavoidable. ZERO.Prmptr's output should feed directly and visibly into the build tool. The Documentation block needs to be a required, guided step — not optional.

4. **Design direction is the unsolved problem for creative teams.** Every technical tool assumes text-first communication. Creative teams think visually. The platform that solves design direction will win the creative-agency audience.

5. **Post-event is an untapped opportunity.** 69% of participants wanted to keep building. A designed "what's next" path converts a one-time event into a recurring capability — and a recurring revenue stream.

---

## Open Strategic Question for Session 3

**B2B platform vs. facilitated program:** The Seven2 event was heavily facilitated — and that's probably what produced the 64% completion rate. Before building the product roadmap, the go-to-market model needs a decision: Is Hacksathon primarily a self-serve SaaS platform, or a service-plus-software facilitation product? The answer shapes the entire feature roadmap.
