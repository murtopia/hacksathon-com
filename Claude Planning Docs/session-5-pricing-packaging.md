## Confirmed Product Decisions

The following pricing decisions are locked and should be treated as constraints, not variables, in all subsequent product and go-to-market work:

1. **Per-event pricing only.** No subscriptions, no annual tiers, no renewals. Every purchase is a one-time event transaction. The product's core value is a permanent capability unlock — most customers will run one event, and the pricing model reflects that reality.

2. **Seat count is the pricing variable.** Packages are based on the number of participants invited to the event, not total company headcount. A large enterprise running the format for a 30-person department buys the same package as a 30-person independent agency.

3. **AI usage is fully absorbed.** AI cost per participant (ZERO.Prmptr Planning Block, IdeaLab scope validation) is under $0.15/person. All packages include AI features with no caps, no overages, no usage limits surfaced to the buyer.

4. **Feature parity across all packages.** Every package includes the complete platform experience. No feature gates between tiers. The format is the format.

5. **Returning customers get 20% off automatically.** Applied at checkout when a prior completed event is on the account. No code required.

6. **Minimum event size: 10 participants.** Below this, the social dynamics that make the format work — peer accountability, showcase energy, Demo Day celebration — start to thin out. Ten is the floor for a meaningful event.

7. **Discount and access code tooling will be built into the platform.** The Organizer will have a code management interface to issue percentage-off discount codes (for affiliate partners, promotional campaigns, and returning-customer bonuses) and fully free access codes (for beta companies, launch partners, and select pilot customers). See Section 6 below.

## The Strategic Reframe (from original pricing model)

The original pricing model assumed repeat customer behavior that the product's own value proposition makes unlikely. Hacksathon's core outcome is a permanent capability unlock — once a team has shipped their first app, the ceiling is lifted for good. Most customers will run one event. Building subscription tiers and annual renewals around a one-time transformative event is the wrong architecture.

The revised model is built on three principles:

**One event, one price.** Every purchase is a per-event transaction. No renewals, no annual commitment, no subscription to manage.

**Seat count is the pricing variable.** The number of participants invited to the event determines which package applies. A 30-person division inside a Fortune 500 buys the same package as a 30-person independent agency — the band model handles every buyer type without custom pricing conversations.

**AI is absorbed, not metered.** AI cost per participant is under $0.15 per person. At these margins, surfacing usage limits or overages would create anxiety mid-event for a cost that is rounding error. All packages include AI features with no caps.

## The Package Structure

### Minimum event size: 10 participants

Ten people is the floor for several reasons. Below this number, the social dynamics that make the format work — peer accountability from the Shark Tank pitch, genuine energy in the showcase, the collective celebration of Demo Day — start to thin out. A 6-person hackathon is a workshop. A 10-person hackathon is an event. The minimum also keeps the economics clean without creating awkward sub-$850 transactions.

### The bands

| Package | Participants | Per seat | Event total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boutique | 10–25 | $85 | $850 – $2,125 |
| Agency | 26–50 | $80 | $2,080 – $4,000 |
| Studio | 51–100 | $70 | $3,570 – $7,000 |
| Large | 101–200 | $65 | $6,565 – $13,000 |
| 200+ | Contact us | — | — |

**How bands work:** The buyer selects the package that fits the number of participants they're inviting to the event — not their total company headcount. A 200-person agency sending 35 people to the hackathon buys the Agency package. A large enterprise running the format for a 28-person creative division buys the Agency package. The model handles every real-world scenario without custom pricing conversations until genuinely outlier scale (200+).

**Per-seat pricing decreases with scale.** Larger events have lower marginal cost to serve and the buyer's per-person cost drops as a natural reward for bringing more participants. The decrease is modest (Boutique to Large is $85 → $65, a 24% reduction) — enough to feel fair, not enough to create perverse incentives to inflate participant counts.

**The "Contact us" at 200+** exists because at genuine enterprise scale there are real logistical questions — platform load, support needs, potentially a custom onboarding call — that warrant a conversation before purchase. This is not a bespoke enterprise product tier. It is a prompt to talk before taking their money. The outcome is typically a Large package at a negotiated rate, or a custom arrangement for multi-department rollouts.

Every package includes the complete platform experience. The format is the format — a Boutique buyer should not receive a stripped-down version of what a Studio buyer gets.

## Returning Customer Discount

**20% off any subsequent event, applied automatically at checkout.**

No code required. When an Organizer account has a completed prior event on record, the discount applies automatically at checkout on their next purchase. The buyer does not need to ask for it or remember a code.

The 20% discount is meaningful (a 25-person returning customer saves $425) without being so deep that it creates incentives to game the system. Running a minimal event just to establish returning-customer status is not worth the setup cost at the 10-person minimum.

This also handles the edge case of companies who do run a second event — a new cohort, a new department, a leadership team wanting their own version. The format may be "one and done" for most buyers, but the 20% discount makes the second event a no-brainer if the context arises.

## Add-Ons

### Launch Partner Package — $1,500

Available on any package. Nick's direct involvement in a defined, time-bounded format:
- Pre-event: 90-minute kickoff call (recorded and shareable with the full leadership team). Nick reviews the Organizer's event configuration, suggests custom block descriptions, and advises on participant framing for their specific company culture.
- During event: Async support via email/Slack for four weeks.
- Mid-event: One optional 30-minute check-in call.

This is not facilitation. The platform facilitates. This is access to the person who designed the format — setup support and a confidence layer for first-time Organizers. **Limited to the first 20 clients.**

### "Vibe Coding for Creatives" — $29 / $199 org license

Standalone book, sold separately. Every copy includes a $100-off code for a first Starter event. The org license ($199 for 25 digital copies) is designed for Organizers who want to distribute the book to all participants before the kickoff — it pre-loads the mindset work that Block 1 (Kickoff) would otherwise carry entirely.

Companion pieces (Idea Worksheet, Prompt Template, Vibe QA Checklist, Build Tracker, Demo Framework) are always free and ungated at hacksathon.com/resources.

## Free Experience — Organizer Preview

No freemium tier. No time-limited free trial. Instead: any Organizer can create a free account and configure a complete event — all 10 blocks, custom branding, awards categories, block descriptions — and preview every participant-facing screen and coaching tip, without being able to invite participants.

The Preview converts to a paid event when the Organizer clicks "Invite Participants" and enters payment. This is the highest-conversion moment in the purchase journey: the Organizer has already built their event and has real sunk cost in the configuration. Abandoning means starting over elsewhere.

## Discount Codes and Free Access — Beta and Promotional Tooling

### The tool

Nick will need a lightweight code management interface (within the Murtopolis admin panel) to issue and track two types of codes:

**Percentage-off discount codes**
- Configurable discount percentage (e.g., 10%, 20%, 50%)
- Can be single-use or multi-use (e.g., an influencer's affiliate code used by many buyers)
- Optional expiration date
- Applies at checkout, stacks with or replaces the automatic returning-customer discount (configurable)
- Use cases: affiliate partner codes, campaign promotions, conference giveaways, partnership deals

**Free access codes (100% off)**
- Single-use, tied to a specific package tier or any tier
- Used for beta companies, launch partners, press access, and select pilot customers who Nick wants to give full access to the platform in exchange for feedback, a testimonial, or case study rights
- Generates a full paid event experience — the beta company sees no indication they are on a free code; the experience is identical to a paying customer

### Beta program strategy

Issuing free codes to a select group of companies before public launch serves several purposes:

- **Product validation** at real event scale, with real teams, before money changes hands
- **Case study pipeline** beyond Seven2 — multiple industries, company sizes, and team compositions
- **Word-of-mouth seeding** — beta companies who ran a successful event become the most credible advocates
- **Testimonial and quote capture** — reflection data from beta events can feed the marketing site and case study page

Suggested beta criteria: companies that represent the ICP (creative agencies, 20–50 people), are willing to share reflection data and a post-event debrief with Nick, and have at least one internal champion who will actively facilitate the event. Beta access is a value exchange, not a freebie.

### MVP scope for code tooling

The code management tool does not need to be complex at launch. In MVP, a simple admin interface in Murtopolis that allows Nick to:

1. Create a code (type: percentage-off or free, value, usage limit, expiration)
2. Copy the code to share manually (email, DM, document)
3. View a list of created codes and their usage status (created / used / expired)

Self-serve affiliate code generation (where an influencer logs in and generates their own link) is a Growth feature — not needed until the affiliate program is active at scale.

## Distribution and Commission Model

### Affiliate and influencer partnerships — 10–15%

The primary distribution channel beyond direct and organic is affiliate and influencer marketing targeting the agency and creative professional audience. Creators with agency-focused audiences (marketing operations YouTubers, design community newsletters, agency leadership podcasters) receive a unique referral link and earn 10–15% of completed purchases attributed to their link.

**Platform recommendation:** PartnerStack for tracking, attribution, and automated payouts. Handles fraud protection and 1099 compliance. Platform fee approximately 2–3% of commission paid.

**Rate rationale:** 10–15% is the market rate for SaaS affiliate programs in the productivity and team tools space. Below 10% and creators will deprioritize the product in favor of higher-commission alternatives. Above 15% compresses margins meaningfully, especially on Boutique packages.

### Referral partners — 10%

For agencies, consultants, or L&D professionals who refer Hacksathon to their clients or network without actively promoting it, a flat 10% referral rate is appropriate. This is a "tell a friend" rate — rewarding the warm intro without expecting ongoing promotional effort.

### No internal sales hire (at launch)

The first 20–30 customers should come from Nick's direct network and the Seven2 case study doing the talking. Once a repeatable inbound playbook is established, the reseller or partner conversation can be revisited. Putting 15% in the margin model as a planning assumption is sufficient for now.

### Margin analysis with commission built in

At 15% commission (the ceiling of the affiliate range), margins are healthy at every tier:

| Package | Avg transaction (midpoint) | 15% commission | AI cost | Net |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Boutique (17 seats) | $1,445 | $217 | $3 | ~$1,225 |
| Agency (38 seats) | $3,040 | $456 | $6 | ~$2,578 |
| Studio (75 seats) | $5,250 | $788 | $11 | ~$4,451 |
| Large (150 seats) | $9,750 | $1,463 | $23 | ~$8,264 |

There is no scenario in the current pricing structure where commission creates a profitability problem.

## What This Means for Enterprise Customers

Large enterprise buyers are not a separate tier — they are department and division buyers who happen to work at big companies. A VP at a Fortune 500 running Hacksathon for their 30-person creative team buys the Agency package. A regional director running it for 60 people buys the Studio package. No custom pricing conversation, no enterprise sales cycle.

The band model handles large-company buyers at the division level without any additional product or pricing work. The only time a genuine enterprise conversation is warranted is if a central L&D or innovation team wants to run the format across multiple departments simultaneously — and that is a Large package purchase, potentially with the Launch Partner add-on for coordination support.

## Pricing Page Design Guidance

**Lead with the event outcome, not the feature list.** Each band should open with a one-line framing of who it's for:
- Boutique: *"For teams of 10–25. The sweet spot for most first runs."*
- Agency: *"For teams of 26–50. The most common size for a company-wide event."*
- Studio: *"For departments of 51–100. Bring multiple teams into the same event."*
- Large: *"For 100–200 participants. Our largest standard package."*
- 200+: *"Running this for 200+ people? There are some logistics worth talking through first."*

**Show the per-seat price prominently.** Buyers instinctively sanity-check per-seat costs against other team tool investments. $65–$85/seat for a 6-week capability program compares favorably to almost anything in their software budget.

**Surface the returning customer discount.** One sentence below the pricing table: *"Already ran a Hacksathon? Your next event is automatically 20% off at checkout."*

**FAQ on the pricing page (per Session 4 IA) answers three questions:**
- *"What's included in each plan?"* — Link to detailed feature list; answer is "everything"
- *"Can I run a trial event?"* — Explain the Organizer Preview; frame it as a feature, not a consolation
- *"Is facilitation included?"* — *No. The platform facilitates. You run it. (That's the point.)*

## Open Questions for Future Sessions

**Reseller model for agencies selling to clients:** If an agency wants to run Hacksathon *for* their own clients — positioning it as a service they offer — the current model doesn't cleanly accommodate that. A reseller arrangement where the agency buys at a discount and marks up is a different commercial relationship than an affiliate. Worth a dedicated conversation when the first such agency surfaces.

**AppSumo or launch platform strategy:** A one-time AppSumo listing could drive significant launch visibility, but AppSumo's revenue share (30–70%) is aggressive and the buyer profile skews toward deal-hunters rather than agency decision-makers. Probably not the right channel — but worth a deliberate decision rather than leaving it open.

**Self-serve affiliate code generation:** When the affiliate program reaches meaningful scale, influencers and partners should be able to generate and track their own referral links without involving Nick. This is a Growth feature in the Murtopolis admin panel — not needed at launch.
