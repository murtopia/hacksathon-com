## Standing Product Decisions (Confirmed Through Session 5)

Before the GTM strategy, the locked constraints that shape every channel and messaging decision:

- **Self-serve SaaS** — no facilitation dependency at launch. No personal involvement from Nick in onboarding or running events. The platform is the facilitator.
- **Per-event pricing only** — $85–$65/seat depending on band (10-person minimum)
- **Feature parity** — no tier-based feature gates; the format is the format
- **ICP: ad agencies and creative agencies, 20–200 people** — non-technical teams. Professional services firms (law, accounting, consulting) are a confirmed adjacent ICP.
- **The format is the product** — every marketing message serves the emotional arc: *Intimidation → Permission → First output → Surprise → Pride → "I have more ideas"*
- **Launch Partner add-on is removed** — no personal involvement from Nick in onboarding. The Organizer Coaching Layer inside the platform is the entire facilitation experience.
- **100% completion rate at Seven2** — every participant who started, shipped. This is the headline stat, not 64%.

## 1. Launch Plan

### What MVP Launch Actually Means

The goal of launch is not scale. It is **proof of repeatability**. Seven2 proved the format works once. Launch proves it works without Nick's involvement — at companies you've never heard of, run by Organizers who found you through Google. That is the definition of product-market fit for Hacksathon.

The launch sequence has three phases:

**Phase 1: Private Beta (6–8 weeks before public launch)**
Run 3–5 beta events with free access codes across companies that represent the ICP. All beta partners are personally recruited by Nick. The goal is case studies that prove the format transfers without facilitation. See Section 6 for the complete Beta Partner Recruitment Strategy.

**Phase 2: Soft Launch (invite-only)**
Open self-serve purchasing to a waitlist or email list built during beta. No paid ads yet. Direct network outreach. The Seven2 case study is live. The goal is the first 10 paying customers.

**Phase 3: Public Launch**
ProductHunt, LinkedIn campaign, outreach to agency-focused media. First affiliate partners activated. The public gallery launches only after 6 completed events — not before. The goal is the first 30 customers and a second strong case study beyond Seven2.

### Beta Program Structure

See Section 6 for the complete Beta Partner Recruitment Strategy. Key parameters:

- **3–5 companies maximum** — quality beta, not volume beta
- **All beta partners personally recruited by Nick**
- **Free access via code from Murtopolis code management tool**
- **Hard launch gate:** Two or more events hit 50%+ completion with zero escalations to Nick

### Launch Marketing Channels

**Channel 1: LinkedIn (owned, free)**
The highest-leverage channel for this ICP. A dedicated Hacksathon business account is created — not Nick's personal account. Multiple team members can contribute. Personal accounts amplify and share from the business account. See Section 3 for the full LinkedIn content strategy.

**Channel 2: ProductHunt**
Launch only after the platform is fully built, the Seven2 case study is live, and at least one additional case study beyond Seven2 is ready. Do not launch cold. Time for a Tuesday or Wednesday. Build the hunter network and supporter list in advance. The pitch: *"The hackathon platform for teams who don't code."*

**Channel 3: Direct Outreach (Nick's network)**
The first 10 paying customers come from Nick's personal and professional network. This is a validation channel, not a scalable one. Each direct-network sale should include a request for a testimonial and an open invitation to refer others.

**Channel 4: Agency-Focused Media and Newsletters**
After the Seven2 case study is live, pitch it to newsletters and podcasts with agency-leader audiences. Earned media placements, not ads. The story is inherently interesting: non-technical creative team, 100% completion rate, zero coding experience. Target: *Agency Spotter*, *Campaign*, *The Drum*, agency operations newsletters, marketing leadership podcasts. Lead with the people and outcomes; don't pitch the software.

**Channel 5: Affiliate Partners (at public launch)**
Activate 2–3 affiliate partners at launch. Look for creators with agency-focused audiences already talking about AI tools for creatives. The referral link program is live at public launch; self-serve affiliate code generation is a Growth feature.

**What is NOT a launch channel:**
- Google Ads — wait until you know which keywords convert
- AppSumo — wrong buyer profile (deal-hunters vs. agency decision-makers). Confirmed pass.
- Cold email at scale — reserved for post-launch when proof portfolio is stronger

## 2. The Seven2 Case Study

### Headline Stat

**100% completion rate. Zero coding experience.**

Every single participant who started, shipped. This is the number that goes above the fold on the case study page, the pricing page, and every LinkedIn post.

### How to Package the Seven2 Experience

The Seven2 case study is the most important marketing asset at launch. It should be treated as a product, not a page. It lives at `/case-study/seven2/`, has its own distinct visual treatment, and its own distribution plan.

**The framing principle:** Don't lead with the tools. Don't lead with the platform. Lead with the people and the moment. A creative director who made bedtime stories for her kids. A strategist who figured out she could be a product architect. A project manager who shipped a cybersecurity tool for non-technical teams.

**Case study structure:**

1. **Hero stat, above the fold:** *"100% completion rate. Zero coding experience."*
2. **The Challenge:** Creative agency, 22 people, leadership wanted AI fluency without hiring a developer. No one had written a line of code.
3. **The Format:** Six weeks, part-time. Time-blocked 30–45 minute sessions. Shark Tank pitch. Demo Day. Awards ceremony.
4. **The Results:** Metrics table. Lead with the human numbers: 14 live apps shipped, 9 of 13 participants wanted to keep building.
5. **The Unexpected Win:** The top three projects were all personal — not work tools. This is the counterintuitive insight that makes the case study memorable and shareable.
6. **Featured Projects:** Drift, Cut-up Lyric Generator, Even Grounds. First name only. Screenshots. Live URLs if active.
7. **Participant Quotes:** 5–6 high-impact quotes (see below).
8. **The Lasting Impact:** 69% wanted to keep building. A capability shift, not a one-time output.
9. **CTA:** *"Run this at your company."*

### The Most Powerful Quotes

**Lead quote — opens the case study, use everywhere:**
> *"I have definitely found myself thinking, 'oh I could make a solution for that' — rather than 'someone should make an app for that.'"* — Sena Lauer, Seven2

**The accessibility proof (for skeptical buyers):**
> *"Hacks-a-thon definitely proved that anyone can make an app or website using current AI and vibe coding platforms."* — Adam Simons

**The surprise moment (emotional resonance):**
> *"I hardly had to do anything manually! I was expecting to tweak more myself with design aspects, but it was so seamless."* — Alliyah Evans

**The mindset shift:**
> *"You don't have to choose between being a designer or a developer — you can be a product architect."* — Joe Moore

**The practical advice (most shareable):**
> *"Just start! And refine."* — Callen Fulbright

**The unexpected human moment (best for social — self-aware and funny):**
> *"I would make a great client — the prompts I gave Lovable felt like a client + agency relationship."* — Eiser

**The organizer reflection:**
> *"How much fun everybody else had."* — Nick Murto (on what surprised him most as the organizer)

### Metrics to Highlight

| Metric | How to frame it |
|---|---|
| 100% completion rate | Lead with it. Every person who started, shipped. |
| 14 live apps shipped | Pair with: "None of them had ever written a line of code." |
| ~2 technical participants | Reframe: "The other 20 had no prior coding experience." |
| 69% wanted to keep building | The durable outcome — not just a one-time event. |
| 6 weeks, part-time | 30–45 minute sessions. Makes it feel achievable. |
| Best in Show: 12 of 15 votes | Illustrates quality, not just completion. |

**The metric to avoid leading with:** Total participant count (22 is small). Frame it as "a full company run" instead.

## 3. Content Marketing

### LinkedIn Strategy

A dedicated Hacksathon business account is the primary publishing surface. Multiple team members can contribute and publish. Personal accounts amplify from the business account.

**Four content pillars, rotating:**

**Pillar 1: The Story Posts**
Long-form LinkedIn posts about individual participants from Seven2. Not "here's a case study." Narrative format: *"She's a creative director at a 22-person agency. She had never written a line of code. Six weeks ago she shipped a bedtime story app for her kids."* These get shared. They create the emotional connection that a stats-forward case study can't.

**Pillar 2: The Insight Posts**
Short, provocative observations drawn from the Seven2 data:
- *"The top 3 projects at our hackathon were all personal. None were work tools. This was not an accident."*
- *"100% of non-technical people shipped live apps in 6 weeks. The #1 thing they had in common: they planned before they prompted."*
- *"One participant said she 'would make a great client.' She's now a product architect."*

**Pillar 3: The Practical Posts**
Useful, specific content about running AI capability programs at non-technical companies:
- *"Why personal passion projects outperform work projects in hackathons."*
- *"The one sentence that unlocked creative building for non-technical teams."*
- *"How we got a 100% completion rate in a part-time hackathon. (The answer isn't tools.)"*

**Pillar 4: The Behind-the-Build Posts**
Short updates on building Hacksathon.com itself. Authentic engagement from the indie maker and no-code community. Positions the product as built by a builder, not just marketed by a marketer.

**Post cadence:** 3–4 posts per week. One long story post (Pillar 1) per week. Two short insight or practical posts. One behind-the-scenes post.

**The repurposing chain:** Every LinkedIn post that performs well becomes a seed for a longer blog post, a quote card for the case study, or content for agency media outreach.

### Blog Topics

**SEO acquisition (people actively searching):**
- *How to run an internal hackathon for non-technical teams*
- *AI team training that actually works (not another workshop)*
- *How to build AI fluency at a creative agency*
- *Vibe coding for non-developers — what it is and why it matters*
- *How to run a company hackathon without engineers*
- *Internal innovation programs that don't die after the kickoff*

**Authority building (people evaluating after finding you):**
- *Why passion projects outperform work projects in hackathons*
- *What we learned from 22 non-technical people shipping 14 live apps*
- *The planning block: why we gate the build behind 30 minutes of documentation*

- *The emotional arc of a hackathon: from intimidation to "I have more ideas"*
- *Why time-blocking is the product: what calendar-protected sessions do that self-directed learning can't*

**Post-launch (as more events generate data):**
- *What the second event teaches you that the first one can't*
- *The Hacksathon.com showcase: everything teams have built*
- *How [Company X] ran a hackathon at a law firm / accounting firm*

**Publishing cadence:** One post per week. Quality over volume. Every post should be specific, opinionated, and genuinely useful.

## 4. Sales Strategy

### Self-Serve Only

The ICP makes purchasing decisions at the CMO or agency head level. These buyers are comfortable with software-led purchasing. The goal is to make the self-serve path so clear that no buyer at the Boutique or Agency tier ever feels like they need to talk to a human before purchasing.

The Contact form exists for genuine enterprise scale (200+ participants) — not as a crutch for a weak product page.

**Two moments where self-serve can break down — and the solutions:**

1. *"I want to see it before I buy."* — The Organizer Preview solves this. A read-only walkthrough of the Participant experience and Organizer dashboard. Not a free trial — a demo environment.
2. *"I need to justify this to my CMO/CFO."* — The Seven2 case study page, the per-seat pricing framing ($65–$85/seat), and the Event Summary report from a completed event all serve this need.

### Target Customer Acquisition Channels

**Channel 1: Nick's direct network (first 10 customers)**
Every person in Nick's professional network who leads a creative or ad agency, or heads L&D at a knowledge-work company, is a warm prospect. The pitch: link to the Seven2 case study. No deck needed.

**Channel 2: Seven2 participants as advocates**
The 22 Seven2 participants will move to other agencies and companies. Some will become CMOs and L&D leads. They have lived proof. Make it easy for them to share the case study and refer their new companies. A simple referral email template sent to all Seven2 participants post-launch costs nothing and activates a high-conversion word-of-mouth channel.

**Channel 3: Ad agency directories and trade media**
Agency Spotter, Clutch, The Drum's agency rankings. These are findable, targetable audiences with the exact buyer profile. Earned media placements in agency trade publications are more valuable than ads at this stage.

**Channel 4: Build tool partnerships (see below)**

**Channel 5: Organic search (12–18 month horizon)**
Start publishing immediately. Don't rely on organic search for the first six months.

### Partnership Opportunities

**Partnership with Lovable (highest priority)**
Lovable is already the default tool with affiliate link attribution. Formalize it:
- **Affiliate link program:** Every Lovable account created through a Hacksathon event is attributed and earns a referral fee
- **Co-marketing:** Joint LinkedIn content: *"We ran a hackathon with 22 non-technical people. Here's what they built with Lovable."*
- **Hacksathon in Lovable's documentation:** A listing or mention in Lovable's team-use or getting-started resources

**Partnership with Cursor, Bolt, Replit (secondary)**
Same logic, lower priority. These tools skew more technical than the primary ICP. Worth pursuing after the Lovable partnership is established.

**What to avoid:** Partnerships that require platform exclusivity or compromise the tool-agnostic positioning.

### Reseller Model — Off the Table

Word-of-mouth referrals will happen organically. No formal reseller or agency-as-reseller structure is needed or expected. Confirmed pass.

### AppSumo — Confirmed Pass

AppSumo's revenue share (30–70%) is aggressive and the buyer profile skews toward deal-hunters rather than agency decision-makers. Not the right channel. Confirmed pass.

## 5. Public Gallery and Community

### The Public Gallery

A curated, public-facing gallery of Best in Show winners from across all Hacksathon events. Inspired by ProductHunt's discovery mechanic — anyone can browse, like, and vote. No account required to engage; account required to submit.

**Launch threshold:** The gallery goes live only after **6 completed events** with opted-in projects. Before that, the gallery page exists as a teaser: *"Coming soon — the best of what teams are building with Hacksathon."*

**What shows on each project card:**
- Project name
- Builder's first name + role
- One-line description
- Live URL
- Company name
- Which award category they won

**Voting model:** Cumulative lifetime votes. Projects bubble up by vote count, with recency as a tiebreaker so new events aren't permanently buried by older ones. No daily reset needed at Hacksathon's current scale — revisit if the leaderboard becomes stale over time.

**Consent and privacy:**
Two levels of opt-in, with the more restrictive setting winning:
1. **Company-level default** — the Organizer sets their event as public or private during setup
2. **Project-level opt-in** — each participant individually opts in to public sharing at the Awards stage

The Awards opt-in framing: *"Share this project publicly on the Hacksathon gallery? Your project could inspire builders at other companies."*

This protects companies building sensitive internal tools while making it easy for companies who want visibility to share.

**Only Best in Show winners are featured in the public gallery** at this time. Other award categories may be added later.

### Company Results Page

Every completed event generates a shareable company results page. Designed to be screenshot-worthy and LinkedIn-ready. Contains:
- Company logo and event name
- Total projects shipped and completion rate
- Award winner cards with project names and one-liners
- A single CTA at the bottom: *"Run this at your company → hacksathon.com"*

This is the organic acquisition flywheel: every time an agency CMO shares their results page on LinkedIn, it reaches their entire professional network — other agency leads who see it and think *"we should do this."*

### Community — No Cross-Company Community

There is no global community product. Company A's team does not need to talk to Company B's team. Hacksathon is an internal experience — the community is the team.

Per-event communication happens through dedicated channels (Slack or Teams) set up by the Organizer for their own participants. This is a communications feature, not a community product.

The only cross-company surface is the public gallery — intentionally passive. You browse and vote; you don't converse.

## 6. Beta Partner Recruitment Strategy

### What the Beta Is — and What It Isn't

The beta is a **value exchange with a specific outcome on both sides.**

**Hacksathon gets:** Product validation at real event scale without Nick's involvement, seed content for the public gallery (minimum 6 events needed), company results pages that become launch marketing assets, and reflection data and quotes for case studies beyond Seven2.

**The company gets:** A complete Hacksathon event — full platform access, all 10 blocks, the Awards ceremony — at no cost, plus the distinction of being among the first companies publicly featured on the Hacksathon gallery.

### Beta Criteria

All three required. Non-negotiable.

**1. Matches the ICP**
Ad agency, creative agency, or professional services firm. 15–75 people. Non-technical team.

**2. Has an internal champion**
One person who will own the event — configure it, send the invites, nudge participants, run Demo Day. Not a CEO who will delegate it to an assistant and disappear.

**3. Willing to participate in the value exchange**
- Share post-event reflection data
- 30-minute post-event debrief call with Nick
- At minimum one participant quote for marketing use (opt-in, participant-level)
- Opt-in to public sharing for their Best in Show project on the gallery

### Beta Recruitment

**All beta partners are personally recruited by Nick.** This is a deliberate decision — Nick knows what a good beta partner looks like better than any inbound process will. The recruitment conversations will also surface product questions that can't be anticipated from a desk.

**The pitch message:**

> *"I built a product called Hacksathon — a structured AI hackathon platform designed specifically for non-technical teams. We ran the first one at Seven2 and 100% of participants shipped a live app in six weeks. None of them had ever written code.*
>
> *I'm looking for [3–5] companies to run a free beta event before we publicly launch. You get the full platform at no cost. In exchange, I'd love a 30-minute debrief after your event and permission to feature your team's best project on the Hacksathon gallery.*
>
> *Given your team, I think this would be a genuinely great experience. Want to see what Seven2 built?*"

The last line links to the Seven2 case study. Let the case study close the conversation.

**Beta partner recruitment checklist:**
1. Identify target list — ad agencies and professional services firms with a warm path to the decision-maker or champion
2. Send the pitch, link to the Seven2 case study
3. One short call to confirm fit, get the champion's name and kickoff date
4. Send the confirmation template
5. Hand them the platform

Nick's involvement ends at the confirmation message. The next touchpoint is the 30-minute post-event debrief.

**Beta target profile:**
- Ad agencies and creative agencies, 20–60 people
- Professional services firms (law, accounting) where leadership has expressed AI curiosity — the passion project framing applies universally; these teams will build personal projects, not work tools, just like Seven2
- Companies where Nick has a warm relationship with someone at the decision-making level

**What to avoid:**
- Companies going through leadership transitions or major client crises
- Companies where the "champion" is junior without authority to protect time-blocked sessions
- Companies that have already tried and abandoned an internal AI initiative

### Beta Confirmation Template

> *"Great talking through this. Confirming you're in as one of our [X] beta partners for Hacksathon.*
>
> *Here's what to expect:*
> *— I'll send you platform access and an Organizer onboarding link within [X days]*
> *— The platform walks you through everything — no calls needed to get started*
> *— After your Demo Day, we'll schedule a 30-minute debrief*
> *— Your team's Best in Show project will be featured on the Hacksathon gallery when we launch publicly*
>
> *The only thing I ask before we start: confirm the name of your internal champion and your target kickoff date.*
>
> *Excited to see what your team builds."*

### Beta Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | What it means if missed |
|---|---|---|
| Completion rate per event | ≥ 50% without Nick's involvement | Self-serve facilitation layer needs more work |
| Block 4 (Planning) completion rate | ≥ 80% of participants | The gate mechanic isn't working |
| Organizer support requests | Zero to one per event | Organizer onboarding flow has gaps |
| Gallery opt-in rate | ≥ 1 Best in Show per event | Consent mechanic or framing needs adjustment |
| Champion satisfaction (debrief) | Would recommend to a peer | Product is not yet self-sufficient |

**Hard launch gate: two or more events hit 50%+ completion with zero escalations to Nick.** Everything below that threshold is a product problem to fix before public launch, not after.

### Beta → Launch Pipeline

By public launch, the beta events should produce:
- 3–5 company results pages ready to go live
- 3–5 Best in Show projects seeding the public gallery (meeting the 6-event threshold with early paid events)
- Reflection quotes from multiple companies beyond Seven2
- At minimum one additional case study draft (law firm or accounting firm)
- Organic LinkedIn posts from at least one beta company sharing their results page

The public launch is not a cold start. It is a reveal.

## 7. Second Case Study Strategy

### Confirmed Targets
- Nick's law firm
- Nick's accountants

### Why These Are Stronger Than a Second Ad Agency

Two case studies in the same industry (ad agencies) proves the format works for one type of company. A law firm and an accounting firm prove it works for **professional services broadly** — a much larger addressable market. The "non-technical team that needs AI fluency" problem is just as acute at a 40-person law firm as at a 40-person ad agency.

### The Passion Project Framing Applies Universally

The passion project model — building something you personally want to exist, not something your company needs — is not specific to creative agencies. It is the permission structure that makes the format work for any non-technical team. A paralegal building a recipe organizer and an accountant building a personal finance tracker are no different from a creative director building a bedtime story app. The professional context is irrelevant.

This becomes a key marketing message: *"It doesn't matter what industry your team works in. The format works because people build better when it's personal."*

### Timing

Run these as beta events or early paid events. Do not wait for them before launching publicly — but have at least one ready as a second case study before or shortly after the ProductHunt launch.
