## The Competitive Landscape

### Category 1: Enterprise Innovation Management Platforms

These are the "serious" enterprise players. They are not direct competitors — they target a different buyer, require procurement, and cost a magnitude more — but they define the high end of what companies spend to foster team innovation.

**Brightidea**
- Targets: Enterprise (500+ employees), corporate innovation teams
- Pricing: $20,000–$100,000+/year (custom contracts)
- What it does: Innovation management, idea tracking, challenge campaigns
- Comparison: Price, complexity, and procurement overhead make this a non-starter for agencies. Designed for ideation pipelines, not for teams that want to ship something.

**Spigit (now Planbox)**
- Targets: Large enterprise
- Pricing: $30,000–$150,000/year
- What it does: Crowdsourced idea management at scale
- Comparison: Designed for Fortune 500 innovation pipelines. A 30-person agency buying Spigit would be like buying an aircraft carrier to cross a river.

**IdeaScale**
- Targets: Mid-market to enterprise
- Pricing: $15,000–$50,000/year
- What it does: Idea crowdsourcing, challenge campaigns, community voting
- Comparison: Closer in concept to IdeaLab, but no building component, no format, no AI-native experience.

**The positioning gap:** All of these tools are about collecting ideas. None of them help teams actually build something. Hacksathon.com is the only product in this space where a non-technical participant goes from idea to live URL.

### Category 2: Virtual Event & Workshop Platforms

These platforms handle the mechanics of running events but provide no content, no format, and no AI layer.

**Hopin (now RingCentral Events)**
- Pricing: $99–$799/month for organizers; participant seats additional
- What it does: Virtual event infrastructure — video, stages, networking
- Comparison: This is a venue, not a program. Hacksathon provides the curriculum, structure, and outcomes. You could theoretically run a hackathon inside Hopin, but you'd be building the entire format yourself.

**Butter.us**
- Pricing: Free to $30/seat/month for facilitators
- What it does: Facilitated workshop platform with templates
- Comparison: Excellent for single-session workshops. Not designed for structured, multi-block programs. No AI layer, no build tools integration, no idea-to-product journey.

**Miro**
- Pricing: Free to $16/seat/month
- What it does: Visual collaboration and whiteboarding
- Comparison: Frequently used as a DIY hackathon planning layer. It does none of what Hacksathon does. This is the $0 option that shows up in "we could do this ourselves" conversations.

### Category 3: Team Learning & Development Platforms

HR and L&D tools that corporate buyers sometimes use to frame innovation programming.

**Coursera for Business / LinkedIn Learning**
- Pricing: $15,000–$30,000/year organizational licenses
- What it does: Video courses and certifications for workforce skills
- Comparison: Passive consumption vs. active building. Watching a course on AI tools and actually shipping a product are completely different experiences. No outcomes, no accountability, no showcase.

**General Assembly / Coding Bootcamps**
- Pricing: $3,000–$20,000 per person for intensive programs
- What it does: Teaches coding skills over an extended commitment
- Comparison: Significant time and cost investment per person. Hacksathon gets a non-technical team to shipped products in short, time-blocked sessions with zero prior coding knowledge required — at a fraction of the per-person cost.

### Category 4: The Real Competition — DIY

The most honest competitive analysis acknowledges that the biggest competitor is "we'll just do this ourselves."

**The DIY Stack:**
- Google Forms for idea submission: $0
- Notion for project tracking: $0–$16/month
- Slack for communication: Already paying for it
- Lovable / Cursor / Bolt for building: ~$20–50/month per user
- Someone internally to facilitate: Internal cost only

**Total DIY cost:** ~$0–$100/month in new tooling + significant facilitator time

**Why DIY fails (and why this is the most important section):**

1. **No format.** The time-blocking, the Shark Tank pitch, the scope guardian, the demo day structure — these are the product. You can have all the tools in the world and produce nothing without the format. Seven2 proves this.

2. **No AI-integrated planning.** The ZERO.Prmptr → Builder handoff, the IdeaLab PRD generation, the design direction resources — these don't exist anywhere else as a packaged workflow.

3. **Facilitator cost is real.** Someone has to design the curriculum, manage the blocks, coach participants through blockers, and run the showcase. That's a significant internal time investment. $995 is a bargain against that labor cost.

4. **No accountability structure.** Knowing that 22 peers heard your Shark Tank pitch is what drives completion. A DIY Slack channel doesn't create that accountability.

5. **Outcome data.** Seven2 achieved a 100% completion rate. DIY hackathons without structure typically see 30–50% completion even among technical teams.
